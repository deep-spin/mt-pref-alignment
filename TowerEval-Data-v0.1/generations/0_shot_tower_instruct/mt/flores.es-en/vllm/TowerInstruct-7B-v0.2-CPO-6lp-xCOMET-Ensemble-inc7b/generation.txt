When a small group of living things (a small population) moves away from the main one they came from (for example, if they move to a mountain range or a river, or if they move to a new island so they cannot easily go back) they will often find themselves in a different environment to the one they were used to.
The new environment has different resources and competitors, and therefore a new population will need different characteristics or adaptations than it had before to be a strong competitor.
There have been no changes in the original population, which still require the same adaptations as in the past.
As time passes, as the incipient population adapts to the new environment, it begins to resemble the other population less and less.
Finally, after thousands or even millions of years, the two populations will look so different that they will not be classified as the same species.
This process is called speciation, which simply means the development of new species, and is an inescapable consequence and a fundamental part of evolution.
