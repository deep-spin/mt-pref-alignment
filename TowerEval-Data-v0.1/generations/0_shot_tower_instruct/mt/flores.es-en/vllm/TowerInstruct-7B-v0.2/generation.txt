When a small group of living things (a small population) moves away from the main one they came from (for example, if they move to a mountain range or a river, or if they move to a new island so they can't easily go back) they will often find themselves in a different environment to the one they were used to.
The new environment has different resources and competitors, and so a new population will need different characteristics or adaptations than it had before to be a strong competitor.
There have been no changes in the original population, which still require the same adaptations as in the past.
Over time, as the nascent population adapts to the new environment, it begins to look less and less like the other population.
Finally, after thousands or even millions of years, the two populations will look so different that they cannot be classified as the same species.
This process is called speciation, which simply means the development of new species, and is an inescapable consequence and a fundamental part of evolution.
